Design Guidelines for level designers

🎯

Core Mechanic — Free Connection Model

▼

StarLine uses a free connection model (locked Feb 22). This is the most important design rule:

  • Any star can connect to any star. There are no adjacency constraints.
  • Wrong moves are allowed and remain visible as drawn edges.
  • Target edges define the goal — they are what the player must recreate, not constraints on movement.
  • Win condition: The player's drawn edges exactly match target edges — with zero extras.
✦ This means players create their own traps through wrong assumptions about the shape. The constellation silhouette draws them in; completing the correct edge set is the satisfaction.
⚠ This is NOT a Hamiltonian path game anymore. Don't design levels thinking about "paths." Think about which edges define the shape and which edges players will incorrectly assume are part of it.
✨

Shape = Motivation, Solve = Satisfaction

▼

Every level follows this emotional arc:

  • The constellation silhouette is shown as a faint preview. This is the "hook" — players see the shape and want to create it.
  • The puzzle is figuring out which star-to-star connections form that shape.
  • Wrong assumptions (connecting stars that "look right" but aren't target edges) create discovery moments.
  • Completion triggers a celebration animation. The constellation comes alive.

Design principle: The shape should be immediately recognizable as a real object/animal/symbol. If the constellation doesn't read as what it's supposed to be, redesign it.

📊

Difficulty Scaling

▼

Difficulty comes from two dimensions:

FactorEasyMediumHard
Star count4–67–1011–16
Trap complexity1 obvious trap2–3 traps, layeredMultiple interacting traps
Target edges3–56–1010–18
Target time15–25s25–45s45–90s
✦ Target a 70-80% first-attempt solve rate. If testers consistently fail, the level is too hard for its position. Move it later in the chapter or simplify the trap.
⚠ Never put two hard levels in a row. Follow the wave pattern: easy → medium → hard → easy (relief) → medium → hard.
🎭

Narrative Tiers

▼

Each level falls into one of three narrative tiers:

  • Ambient (majority) — Beautiful shapes, Lumen is delighted. Standard completion flow. Most levels are this.
  • Discovery (chapter-defining) — First of a type, Lumen shows wonder. Special completion text. 2–3 per chapter.
  • Revelation (rare emotional peaks) — Reframes the journey, paired with hardest puzzles. 1 per chapter max.

When designing a level, tag it with its tier. This affects the celebration animation intensity, the fun fact shown at completion, and Lumen's reaction.

🐾

What Makes a Good Constellation Shape

▼

The shape must pass the "squint test" — if you squint at the connected dots, you should immediately recognize the object/animal/symbol.

  • Silhouette clarity: The outline should read clearly at phone screen size. Test at 60% zoom.
  • Star placement: Stars should sit at structural points (joints, tips, corners) — not evenly distributed. An animal should have stars at its head, tail, legs, not along its spine.
  • Edge economy: Use the minimum edges needed for recognition. Extra edges that don't improve readability become confusion traps (bad) rather than design traps (good).
  • Trap integration: The shape should naturally create at least one "obvious wrong connection." A star near another star that looks like it should connect but isn't part of the target.
Good: A dolphin with stars at nose, dorsal fin, tail tip, belly curve.
The tail-to-nose edge looks obvious but isn't in the target → natural trap.

Bad: A dolphin with 12 stars evenly spaced along its outline.
No natural traps, no structural clarity, boring to solve.
🌍

Regional Content Strategy

▼

Build a universal backbone + regional objects per UA market:

  • Universal: Objects everyone recognizes — cat, dolphin, heart, tree, crown, etc.
  • Regional (1–2 per market): Tarsier (PH), Kangaroo (AU), Sakura (JP), Eagle (US), etc.
  • Regional objects are sprinkled into chapters (not dedicated chapters). ~20 min per regional level.

Philippines is the soft launch market. Include at least 1 PH-specific constellation (e.g., Tarsier, Jeepney, or Sampaguita flower).

📐

Coordinate System & Export Format

▼

The StarLine Designer exports star positions as normalized coordinates (0–1 range):

stars: [(0.25, 0.30), (0.50, 0.15), (0.75, 0.30), ...]
edges: [(0,1), (1,2), (2,3), ...]

Important Y-axis note: Star Tracer uses canvas coordinates (y=0 at top). Unity uses y=0 at bottom. The integration script applies unity_y = 1 - canvas_y automatically. You don't need to flip coordinates in the designer.

Export formats available:

  • Claude format — paste directly into Claude for validation, trap analysis, and Unity integration
  • Unity C# — ready-to-paste pattern code
  • JSON — structured data for any pipeline

Level Naming Convention

Every level is a named constellation: Cat, Dolphin, Phoenix, etc. On completion, players see: "✦ [Name] Restored" + a one-line fun fact (one-breath read). The name and fact should make the player feel they restored something meaningful to the sky.

🪤

Trap Taxonomy the design moat

Traps are what make StarLine special. Every level should have at least one trap — an "obvious wrong connection" that feels right but isn't part of the target edges. Players discover the correct solution through trial and error. These traps create the "aha!" moment.

🔒 Core Lockout
Player completes the outer ring first and can't reach the center without extra edges.
🌀 Spiral Dead-end
Following the obvious spiral path leads to an isolated star that can't connect back.
⏳ Choke Point
A critical node connects two subgraphs. Reaching it at the wrong time traps you.
💍 Ring Completion
Completing a ring feels satisfying but leaves middle nodes unreachable.
🚩 Wrong Start
The most visually prominent star (nose, top) is the wrong starting point.
🔗 Edge Sharing
Two stars close together look like they should connect but that edge isn't in the target.
☀️ Ray Skipping
Radial patterns where skipping a ray seems fine but creates an unreachable arm.
🌊 Wave Isolation
Parallel curves where staying on one wave leaves the other unvisited.
🏗️ Base-first
Building the foundation first (feels logical) blocks access to upper structures.
⏱️ Center Timing
Visiting the center node too early or too late splits the graph into unreachable halves.

Level Design Workflow from idea to game

Find Reference

Search for a clear silhouette image of the object/animal. Simple outlines work best. Save it to use as overlay.

Design in StarLine Designer

Upload reference image → place stars at structural points → draw target edges → name the level → set trap type.

Test in Designer

Use the built-in play-test mode. Try to solve it yourself. Try the "obvious wrong" path first — does the trap work?

Validate with Claude

Export "Claude format" → paste into Claude → ask for trap analysis, solution paths, difficulty rating. Claude validates the graph theory.

Export & Share

Export as JSON or Unity C#. Share via GitHub PR or paste into a shared doc. Dheeraj integrates into the Unity project.

Target: ~20 minutes per level

Once you're comfortable with the Designer, a level should take about 20 minutes from reference image to validated export. Don't over-polish in the Designer — the Unity integration handles visuals.

Working with Claude AI-assisted level design

Brahma's Claude Setup

Since you have your own Claude account, here's how to get the most from it for StarLine level design:

  • Create a Claude Project called "StarLine Level Design"
  • Upload this Mission Control page and the Design Guidelines as project knowledge
  • In the project instructions, add: "You are helping design levels for StarLine, a constellation puzzle game. Follow the free connection model rules and trap taxonomy documented in the project knowledge."
  • This gives your Claude instance all the context it needs to validate levels, suggest traps, and generate level data.

Prompt Templates for Level Design

Copy-paste these when working with Claude:

1. Validate a level design:

Here's my StarLine level export:
[paste Claude format export from Designer]

Analyze this level: Does the trap work? What's the difficulty? Are there multiple solution paths? Any dead-end issues?

2. Generate a constellation blueprint:

Design a StarLine constellation for a [ANIMAL/OBJECT].
Requirements:
- [N] stars at structural points
- At least 1 natural trap (preferably [TRAP TYPE])
- Target edges that form a recognizable silhouette
- Difficulty: [easy/medium/hard]

Output: star coordinates (0-1 range), target edges, trap description.

3. Difficulty review:

I have these [N] levels designed for Chapter [X]. Here are their star counts and trap types:
[list levels]

Review the difficulty curve. Are there two hard levels in a row? Is the progression wave smooth?

What Claude Can Help With

  • Validating graph theory (are all target edges reachable? are there unintended solutions?)
  • Suggesting trap placements based on star layout
  • Writing fun facts for completion screens
  • Generating constellation blueprints from descriptions
  • Reviewing difficulty curves across a chapter
  • Converting between export formats (JSON ↔ Unity C# ↔ Claude format)
